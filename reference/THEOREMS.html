Double oppositeestablished

${( {\mathcal{C}}^{\mathrm{op}} )}^{\mathrm{op}} = \mathcal{C}$. Reversing all arrows twice is the identity.

Lesson 3; Riehl §1.2.

Contravariant = covariant on the oppositeestablished

A contravariant functor $\mathcal{C} \longrightarrow \mathcal{D}$ is the same data as a covariant functor ${\mathcal{C}}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathcal{D}$.

Lesson 3; Riehl §1.3.

Homomorphisms are functorsestablished

A monoid/group homomorphism $M \longrightarrow N$ is exactly a functor $\mathbf{B} M \longrightarrow \mathbf{B} N$ between their one-object (delooping) categories.

Lesson 2; Riehl §1.3.

Functors preserve isomorphismsto prove

If $A \xrightarrow{f} B$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$ and $F$ is any functor, then $F ( f )$ is an isomorphism (and $F ( f^{- 1} ) = F {( f )}^{- 1}$). One line from functoriality.

Riehl, Lemma 1.3.8 (functors preserve isomorphisms); cf. Exercise 1.3.viii (they need not reflect them).

An inverse is uniqueto prove

A morphism has at most one two-sided inverse; hence "isomorphism" is unambiguous.

Riehl Exercise 1.1.i(ii).

Duality principleto prove / internalize

The dual of any theorem true of all categories is again true of all categories (apply it in ${\mathcal{C}}^{\mathrm{op}}$). One proof, two theorems.

Lesson 3; Riehl §1.2; Mac Lane §II.1.

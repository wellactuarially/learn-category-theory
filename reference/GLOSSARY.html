Category Theory — Glossary

The canonical language of this workspace. Once a term is defined here, every lesson uses it this way.

Composition convention: this workspace composes left-to-right by juxtaposition, written fg and read "f then g" — for A⟶fB, B⟶gC the composite is A⟶fgC. Morphisms are declared in labeled-arrow style A⟶fB, not f:A→B. Most sources use right-to-left g∘f; when carrying a statement over from one, reverse the order.

Core definition

Category
A collection of objects and, between each ordered pair of them, a collection of morphisms, equipped with an associative composition and an identity morphism on each object satisfying the unit law. Knows only arrows and how they compose — no internal structure of objects is assumed.
Avoid: "a kind of set", "a structure" (too vague).
Object
A member of a category's collection ob(C). Treated as a featureless point; all its meaning comes from the morphisms into and out of it.
Morphism (arrow)
A directed arrow A⟶fB with a definite domain (A) and codomain (B). The collection of all such is the hom-collection C(A,B).
Avoid: "function" (a morphism need not be a function; that's only the special case of Set), "map" used loosely.
Composition
The operation sending composable morphisms A⟶fB, B⟶gC to a single A⟶fgC. Defined exactly when codomain meets domain.
Avoid: g∘f (right-to-left order — this workspace composes left-to-right by juxtaposition; see convention note above).
Identity morphism
The distinguished morphism A⟶idAA on each object that leaves any composable morphism unchanged: idAf=f=fidB.
Associativity
The law (fg)h=f(gh) whenever the composites are defined — parenthesization of an arrow-path never matters.
Unit (identity) law
The law that identities are two-sided units for composition: idAf=f=fidB. The most commonly forgotten axiom.

Foundations & size

Collection
A neutral term for an aggregate of objects, broad enough to include aggregates too large to be sets. Made precise by a foundation: it is either a set or a proper class (NBG), or graded by smallness relative to a fixed Grothendieck universe.
Set
A collection that is a legitimate object of the chosen set theory. Under the universe approach, a "small" collection — a member of the fixed Grothendieck universe U.
Proper class
A collection that is not a set, because it is too large — e.g. the class of all sets. Self-membership of such a thing is what Russell's paradox exploits.
Avoid: "big set", "infinite set".
Grothendieck universe (U)
A set closed under every everyday set-forming operation (its existence is equivalent to an inaccessible cardinal). Its members are the small collections; fixing one is Mac Lane's route to making "set vs collection" precise.
Locally small
A category in which every hom-collection C(A,B) is a genuine set (not a proper class).
Small
A category that is locally small and whose objects also form a set. Distinguished from large categories to avoid size paradoxes (e.g. "the category of all categories").

Functors

Functor
A structure-preserving map between categories, C⟶FD: an assignment of objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms (respecting domain and codomain) that satisfies functoriality.
Avoid: "map of categories" (use functor), "transformation" (reserved for natural transformations).
Functoriality
The two laws a functor obeys: it preserves composition, F(fg)=F(f)F(g), and preserves identities, F(idA)=idF(A).
Covariant functor
A functor that keeps the direction of every arrow. The default: an unqualified "functor" means covariant. Its dual — a functor that reverses arrows, equivalently a covariant functor out of the opposite category Cop — is contravariant (developed next lesson).
Cat
The category whose objects are small categories and whose morphisms are functors, with composition of functors and identity functors. (Restricting to small categories keeps it a legitimate large category.)

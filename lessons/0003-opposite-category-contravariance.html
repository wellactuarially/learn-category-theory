The Opposite Category & Contravariance

Reversing every arrow — and the functors that go backwards.

Some constructions naturally run against the arrows: the dual of a vector space, the preimage of a subset, the functions into a fixed object. Category theory captures all of them with one move — reverse every arrow — packaged as the opposite category. The functors that respect this reversal are contravariant, and one of them, the contravariant hom-functor, is the object the Yoneda lemma is about.

1 · The opposite category

Given a category C, its opposite Cop is the category with:

defRiehl, Category Theory in Context, §1.2 (the opposite category and duality); Leinster, Basic Category Theory, §1.2; nLab: opposite category.

  • The same objects as C.
  • Reversed morphisms: Cop(A,B)=C(B,A). An arrow A⟶fB in Cop is exactly an arrow B⟶fA in C.
  • The same identities, and reversed composition (next).
The one subtle point — composition reverses

Take arrows A⟶fB and B⟶gC in Cop — i.e. B⟶fA and C⟶gB in C. Their Cop-composite "f then g" is an arrow A⟶C in Cop, i.e. an arrow C⟶A in C — and the only such arrow at hand is the C-composite gf (in C: C⟶gB⟶fA). So the composite of f then g in Cop is the composite of g then f in C: order flips.

Reversing twice does nothing: (Cop)op=C. A clean concrete case: a poset is a category with at most one arrow x⟶y whenever x≤y; its opposite is the same set with the order reversed, x≥y.

posetPosets as categories and their opposites: Riehl §1.1–1.2; Leinster §1.1. A useful sanity-check that "reverse all arrows" is a real, familiar operation.

2 · Contravariant functors

A contravariant functor F from C to D assigns objects to objects and reverses arrows:

defRiehl §1.3 (functoriality, covariant vs contravariant); Leinster §1.2; nLab: contravariant functor. The standard move is to define contravariance as covariance on the opposite category.

  1. On morphisms it flips direction: an arrow A⟶fB is sent to F(B)⟶F(f)F(A).
  2. Reverses composition: F(fg)=F(g)F(f)
  3. Preserves identities: F(idA)=idF(A).
The definition that earns its keep

A contravariant functor C⟶D is exactly the same data as a covariant functor Cop⟶D. So "contravariant" introduces no new axioms — it is the Lesson 2 definition applied to Cop, where the order-reversal of composition does the work automatically. This is why Cop is worth the bookkeeping. (Unqualified, "functor" still means covariant.)

3 · Contravariance in the wild

  • The dual vector space. (−)∗:Vect⟶Vect sends V to its dual V∗ and a linear map V⟶fW to its transpose W∗⟶f∗V∗ (precompose a functional with f). Direction flips. Doing it twice, the double dual (−)∗∗, is covariant — and the map V⟶V∗∗ is the canonical example of a natural transformation (next lesson).
    dualThe dual-space functor and the natural map to the double dual are the motivating example of naturality in Eilenberg–Mac Lane (1945) and in Riehl §1.4–1.5. We return to it when we define natural transformations.
  • The contravariant powerset. P:Setop⟶Set sends a set to its powerset and a function X⟶fY to the preimage map f−1:P(Y)⟶P(X). Preimage runs backwards — contravariance again.
    powCovariant (image/direct) and contravariant (preimage) powerset functors: Riehl Example 1.3.x. A clean pair showing the same objects can carry both a covariant and a contravariant functor.
  • The contravariant hom-functor. Fix A in a locally small C. Then C(−,A):Cop⟶Set sends an object X to the set C(X,A) and an arrow X⟶fY to "pre-compose with f", h↦fh — a function C(Y,A)⟶C(X,A). Compare the covariant C(A,−) of Lesson 2 (post-compose). A functor Cop⟶Set is called a presheaf, and the Yoneda lemma is the statement that controls these.
    yonedaPresheaves Cop→Set, the representable C(−,A), and the Yoneda lemma: Riehl Ch. 2; Leinster Ch. 4. This functor is the protagonist of your mission's destination.

Why presheaves? — a set varying over a category

That last functor deserves more than its billing as "the thing in the lemma," and its idea is older than category theory's interest in it. In the 1940s Jean Leray, working as a prisoner of war, introduced sheaves to organize locally-defined data on a space. The presheaf part is the bookkeeping of restriction: to each open set U of a space X assign a set — say the continuous functions on U — and to an inclusion U⊆V assign the restriction F(V)⟶F(U). The data runs backwards along inclusions. Since the opens form a poset category O(X), this is exactly a functor O(X)op⟶Set — a presheaf, contravariance built in.

histSheaves/presheaves originate with Jean Leray (1940s); the categorical formulation and the presheaf category are developed by Grothendieck and others. nLab: presheaf; history at nLab: sheaf.

Grothendieck made the move that matters for us: replace O(X) by any category. A presheaf Cop⟶Set is then a generalized, "variable" set indexed by C — and an object A of C appears as one of them, via the representable C(−,A), whose elements over X are the ways of probing A by maps X⟶A. (This is the "functor of points" that organizes modern algebraic geometry.)

The payoff is structural: the presheaf category [Cop,Set] is a rich completion of C — it has all colimits — into which C embeds by A↦C(−,A). The Yoneda lemma is the precise statement that this embedding loses nothing — an object is faithfully described by the maps into it. That is the reason to meet presheaves now, not only at the lemma.

Why it matters — duality

Reversing every arrow sends each categorical notion to its dual: a construction in C is the dual construction in Cop. Because every category has an opposite and (Cop)op=C, any theorem proved for all categories instantly yields a second, dual theorem for free. We cash this in later: limits dualize to colimits, terminal objects to initial ones.

dualThe duality principle: Riehl §1.2; Mac Lane §II.1. One proof, two theorems — a recurring economy in the subject.

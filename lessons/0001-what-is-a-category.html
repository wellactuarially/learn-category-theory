Category Theory · Lesson 1

What is a Category?

The definition, where it came from, and why it conquered modern mathematics.

A category is among the simplest definitions in mathematics and among the most far-reaching. This lesson states it precisely, traces it to the 1945 paper that introduced it, and sets out why it became a common language across the mathematical sciences.

1 · The definition

A category C consists of two kinds of data and three laws they must obey.

defThe formulation here follows Riehl, Category Theory in Context, §1.1, Def. 1.1.1, and agrees with Mac Lane, Categories for the Working Mathematician, §I.2. Riehl PDF · nLab: category.
The data:

  • Objects — a collection ob(C). Write A,B,C,…
  • Morphisms (arrows) — for each ordered pair of objects A,B, a collection C(A,B) of arrows A⟶fB. Every arrow has a definite domain and codomain.
The word "collection"

We say collection rather than "set" because the aggregates above can be too large to be sets. In a foundation like ZFC every object is a set, yet not every property carves out a set: the property "is a set" specifies the class of all sets, which is a proper class — a collection too large to be a set, the very thing Russell's paradox exploits. Collection is the neutral term covering both sets and proper classes.

Two standard ways make this precise: (i) NBG set theory, which admits classes as objects beside sets — a proper class being a class that is a member of nothing; or (ii) fix a Grothendieck universe U — a set so large it is closed under every everyday set-forming operation (its existence is equivalent to an inaccessible cardinal) — and call a collection small exactly when it is a member of U. Mac Lane takes route (ii).

foundOn set-theoretic foundations for category theory — sets vs proper classes, NBG, and Grothendieck universes — see nLab: category theory and foundations; Mac Lane, Categories for the Working Mathematician, §I.6 (Foundations), which fixes one universe; and Riehl §1.1. Background: Small set (category theory).

The laws:

  1. Composition. Given A⟶fB and B⟶gC — i.e. whenever the codomain of one matches the domain of the next — there is a composite A⟶fgC, read "f then g".
  2. Identity. Every object A carries an identity arrow A⟶idAA.
  3. The two axioms these must satisfy:
    • Associativity: (fg)h = f(gh) whenever the composites are defined.
    • Unit (identity) law: idAf = f = fidB for every A⟶fB.
House convention — read this once

We compose left-to-right (the "diagrammatic" order) and write composition by juxtaposition: fg means "do f, then g", so the types line up the way you read — A⟶fB⟶gC. Most sources do the opposite, writing g∘f for the same arrow (function-application order). The content is identical; only the order differs. When you carry a statement over from Mac Lane, Riehl, Leinster, or nLab, reverse the order: their g∘f is our fg. This is the single most common place to slip, so it's worth burning in now.

That's all. No elements, no equations between objects, no internal structure assumed — a category knows only its arrows and how they compose. The discipline of saying everything in terms of arrows is exactly what gives category theory its reach.

A point of precision

Because each C(A,B) is a collection, it may be a proper class rather than a set. When every C(A,B) is a set, C is locally small; when ob(C) is a set as well, C is small. This bookkeeping is what blocks forming "the category of all categories" naively.

sizeOn smallness / local smallness and the size issues that motivate it, see Leinster, Basic Category Theory, §1.1, and Riehl §1.1. Leinster (arXiv).

The picture worth keeping

Think of a directed graph whose edges you're allowed to follow in sequence (compose), where every vertex has a "stay put" loop (identity), and where it never matters how you parenthesize a path. A category is a graph with a compositional structure laid over it.

2 · A little history

Category theory was born in a single 1945 paper: Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane's General Theory of Natural Equivalences.

1945Eilenberg, S. & Mac Lane, S. (1945). "General Theory of Natural Equivalences." Transactions of the AMS 58(2), 231–294. Presented to the Society Sept. 8, 1942; received May 15, 1945. Original PDF (AMS).
Strikingly, the concept they actually wanted was the last of the three they had to define.

Their real target was the word natural. In algebraic topology, certain isomorphisms — like that between a finite-dimensional vector space and its double dual — felt "canonical" in a way others did not, and mathematicians used the word "natural" informally without being able to say what it meant. To make "natural transformation" precise, Eilenberg and Mac Lane first needed functors (maps between the structures being compared); and to define a functor, they first needed categories (the structures themselves).

orderThe often-quoted remark is Mac Lane's: categories were defined in order to define functors, and functors in order to define natural transformations. See Mac Lane, Categories for the Working Mathematician, Ch. I preface; and the historical note in Riehl §1.1. nLab on Eilenberg–Mac Lane.
So the definition you just reviewed was, historically, scaffolding — built to support a notion of "naturality" that we'll reach in a later lesson, and which sits directly on the road to your mission's destination, the Yoneda lemma.

For its first decades category theory was sometimes dismissed as "general abstract nonsense" — a half-affectionate, half-skeptical label for arguments so general they seemed to be about nothing in particular. The phrase stuck even as the subject proved indispensable.

nonsense"General abstract nonsense" is attributed in usage to Steenrod; today it's used affectionately for diagram-chasing/categorical arguments. nLab: abstract nonsense.

3 · Why it took over

The definition is almost insultingly simple, yet category theory became a lingua franca across mathematics and beyond. Three reasons, building on each other:

  • It is everywhere at once. Sets and functions, groups and homomorphisms, vector spaces and linear maps, topological spaces and continuous maps, posets and order-preserving maps, even a single monoid (one object, its elements as arrows) — all are categories. A theorem proved about categories in general is a theorem about all of these simultaneously.
    exThese standard examples are catalogued in Riehl §1.1 (Examples 1.1.3–1.1.4) and Leinster §1.1. The one-object-monoid and poset-as-category examples are the ones that most reliably stretch a beginner's mental model.
  • It defines things by their relationships, not their guts. The categorical style specifies an object by how it maps to and from everything else — a universal property. This is why "the" product, quotient, or free object is characterized up to unique isomorphism without ever opening it up. Spotting these universal properties in the wild is precisely what you set out to do.
    univUniversal properties are the organizing theme of Leinster's book ("At its heart is the concept of a universal property") and the bridge to adjunctions, representables, and limits. Leinster (arXiv).
  • It translates between fields. Because a functor carries one category's structure into another's, category theory became the machinery for moving knowledge across domains — algebraic topology was its first home, and it has since become standard language in programming-language semantics (Lambek's types-as-objects, Moggi's monads), quantum theory, databases, probability, and the broad program now called applied category theory.
    applyOn categories in CS: Lambek (1980) showed typed programs form a cartesian closed category; Moggi imported monads into semantics. The cross-disciplinary program is surveyed under "applied category theory." Applied category theory (overview) · the n-Category Café.

One problem from Emily Riehl's Category Theory in Context, §1.1 — building a category straight from the definition. Answer, then grade with the tutor or reveal the rubric.

The slice category. For a category C and an object c, the slice c/C has as objects the morphisms c⟶fx, and as a morphism from c⟶fx to c⟶gy a map x⟶hy with fh=g (f then h equals g). Verify that c/C is a category. (Riehl, Exercise 1.1.iii(i).)

A slice-morphism is an underlying C-arrow h with fh=g; compose by composing the h's (the commuting triangles paste), the identity is idx, and associativity/units come straight from C. Source: Riehl, Exercise 1.1.iii(i). (Riehl writes g=h∘f; in our left-to-right convention that is fh=g.)
